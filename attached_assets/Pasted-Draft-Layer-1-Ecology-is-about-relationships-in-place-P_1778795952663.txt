Draft
Layer 1 — Ecology is about relationships in place.
Plants, animals, fungi, soil, water, fire, weather, people, history — all interacting in specific places over time. Any ecologically sensitive person already lives in this. The work is about relationships, not facts about species.
Key phrase: relationships in places, not facts about species.
Layer 2 — The decisions that shape these places are mostly made by non-experts.
Most of the land in any region is in the hands of homeowners, small landowners, municipal staff, volunteer boards, weekend stewards. The experts are vastly outnumbered. The day-to-day decisions about what gets planted, removed, burned, mowed aggregate into the ecological reality of the region.
Key phrase: aggregate decision-makers are not the experts.
Layer 3 — People make most decisions alone, regardless of how many experts exist.
Face-to-face is necessary and irreplaceable for some kinds of connection. It is also insufficient. Most decisions in any domain are made alone — at kitchen tables, in front of screens, walking land. Even in domains awash with pundits and forums, people do most of their actual analysis alone. Any system that pretends the alone-channel doesn't exist is missing where most ecological decisions actually happen.
Key phrase: face-to-face is necessary and insufficient; most decisions happen alone.
Layer 4 — The data is broken in three specific ways.
First, we are awash in it — overwhelming volume, contradictory, scattered across countless websites, books, catalogs, forums, agency PDFs. Second, significant sources are walled off, hard to reach, or only accessible through human-browsing interfaces that machines and synthesizers cannot use. Third, the technology of access has changed under us — books, then websites, then spreadsheets, then REST APIs, now MCP — and will keep changing. Each generation of access methods opens what the prior generation could not reach and obsoletes assumptions built on the previous one. Any ecological knowledge that wants to be reachable at scale has to move with the access methods.
Key phrase: too much, walled off, and the methods of access keep moving.
Layer 5 — There is now a new class of tools that changes what is possible.
Broadly called AI, used loosely. A new tool class — like chainsaws, fire, vehicles, propane, herbicides — with real failure modes (confabulation, training bias, confident wrongness) and real new capability (reading across scattered text, synthesizing, answering specifically). Not a replacement for experts. A way to extend expert thinking into the alone-moments where decisions actually happen.
Key phrase: new tool class, real failure modes, genuinely changes what is possible.
Layer 6 — Tools and analysis need findable, well-described, and reachable data sources.
Tools fed marketing copy produce marketing copy. Tools fed Michigan Flora, MNFI, BONAP, FQA analysis, peer-reviewed work, and field work observations produce something useful.
The first construction decision: not another book, not more experts, not a replacement database. Every source kept as itself — Michigan Flora stays Michigan Flora, BONAP stays BONAP, nursery species descriptions stay just that. The commons describes each source (what it covers, how it was made, what its limits are, who maintains it), provides uniform ways to find it, and exposes it through common access methods. Sources retain their identity, authority, and governance. Prior attempts failed by trying to swallow sources into a single rewritten store; this one refuses to.
Key phrase: the tools are downstream of the data.
Key phrase: describe and reach; never absorb.
Layer 7 — The naming layer: every name is a node; every equivalence carries provenance; no naming tradition is canonical.
The second construction decision, and the morally serious one. Existing systems pick scientific Latin as canonical and treat every other name — English vernacular, regional names, indigenous and community names, program-specific names — as decoration. That choice encodes a hierarchy the underlying reality does not support. The naming layer refuses it. Every name is a node in a graph. Every assertion that two names refer to the same living thing is an edge with provenance (who said so, when, on what basis). A species concept is whatever connected component emerges from the graph. The user's input is always the center of the search. Communities can contribute names and equivalences as first-class graph contributions, structurally identical to a major synonymy publisher's. The architecture does not by itself solve the historical inequities in how naming traditions have been documented, but it refuses to encode them into the substrate.
Key phrase: no canonical name; every equivalence is an attributed claim; the user's input is the center.
Layer 8 — The trust layer: named trust scopes filter the data layer's full-provenance output for specific applications and audiences.
The third construction decision. The data layer below carries everything every source and every contributor has asserted, with full provenance on every claim and every equivalence. That is operationally complete but not directly usable — most audiences cannot reason about a maximalist provenance stream, and most applications need to narrow the field before they present anything. The trust layer is where that narrowing happens. Named trust scopes are declared at this layer — peer-reviewed botanical sources only, Michigan-focused regional authorities, named community contributors plus major synonymy publishers, whatever the deployment requires — and each scope is a filter expressed against the provenance metadata flowing up from below. An application running under a given scope sees only the claims and equivalences whose provenance the scope honors. The scopes themselves are declared, attributed, versioned, and visible; a user can ask what scope an answer was filtered through and inspect what was excluded. The data layer below remains neutral and complete. The application layer above does not invent its own trust logic. Trust is a layer, with its own discipline and its own accountability.
Key phrase: trust is a layer of named, declared scopes — applied above the data, exposed to the user, never silently embedded.
Layer 9 — The application layer: tools built on top, each opinionated for a specific audience.
The fourth construction decision. The commons itself is not what anyone uses directly. What people use are applications built on top — species explorers, planting calculators, question-answering tools, research interfaces, regional planners. The commons stays neutral and source-faithful. Applications carry the opinions, the simplifications, the audience-specific trust scopes, the new-tool integration. The separation is what makes the commons sustainable and the applications adaptable to whatever audience and technology comes next.
Key phrase: the commons is neutral; the applications are opinionated.
Layer 10 — The commons architects the ecological, the technical, and the social in reciprocity.
The ecological is the content. A million years of evolution, specialization, and adaptation; organisms in relationship with other organisms in specific places over deep time. The commons treats this as substance, not subject — the reason the architecture exists, not material the architecture is free to reshape. Every layer above is accountable to it.
The technical is the shape and structure. The architectural disciplines that let the commons stay honest about its own job: sources kept as themselves, names without a canonical tradition, trust as a named declared layer, applications as where opinions live. Not the tools, not the code — the structural agreements that determine whether the system is trustworthy. The commons takes its shape from what each layer needs to remain accountable to the layers around it.
The social is the human relationships, the cross-cultural trust, the decision-making, the regional vision. The machinery of how humans across cultures, regions, institutions, and generations decide what counts, who is heard, what is honored, and what direction the whole thing moves in. The commons treats these relationships as load-bearing, not decorative — built into the substrate as named, attributed, accountable contributions rather than assumed at the edges.
None of the three is sufficient alone. The ecological without the technical is unreachable at scale. The technical without the ecological is empty scaffolding. Either without the social is a structure no one is responsible to and no one is responsible for. The commons is what reciprocity among the three produces.
Key phrase: The commons architects the ecological, the technical, and the social in reciprocity.
