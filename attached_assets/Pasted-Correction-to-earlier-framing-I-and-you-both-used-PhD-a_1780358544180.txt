What's findable about Greg
Origin and training. Grew up in Garden City, west of Detroit, when there were still vacant lots and woodlots in the area. Went to U-M, "sampled a number of programs before finally settling into the School of Natural Resources." Bachelor's in Natural Resource Ecology Management. No graduate degree.
Pre-nursery field experience. Immediately after graduating he took a summer position with a graduate student cataloging riverine ecosystems along rivers in Michigan's Huron and Manistee National Forests. The work involved drawing transects from the river edge through banks, levees, floodplains, and upper banks, sampling hundreds of plots and identifying every tree, shrub, and herbaceous plant in each one. This is real field botany at scale.
Notable detail: the group "didn't have an encyclopedic knowledge of the herbaceous plants they were encountering, so late nights were spent learning the plants, keying them out, or preserving the plants they couldn't identify for later identification by plant experts like Tony Reznicek and Ed Voss." Those two names matter. Edward Voss is the author of Michigan Flora (the foundational three-volume work; the database your spec already references). Tony Reznicek is the second author and Voss's successor at the U-M Herbarium. So Greg's specimens were going to the two most authoritative botanists in Michigan for identification. That's not a casual connection — it's direct contact with the people whose work defines Michigan plant identification.
Joined NAP. A year after the riverine work, Greg's friend Bill Schneider (then at Ann Arbor's Natural Areas Preservation program, now owner of Wildtype Native Plant Nursery in Mason) brought him on as a full-time NAP worker. The job: collecting and processing native plant seed from local areas for conservation use. This is where Greg learned Michigan-genotype seed collection from the practitioners doing it for the city.
The founding insight. "Greg tried planting small amounts of this seed in areas around his rental unit and got a thrill from participating in the entire process of seeing the plants in the wild, collecting and processing the seed, and growing the seed into the same plants he had collected them from." That's the seed-to-plant cycle that became the nursery's entire premise.
The nursery. Founded 1998 with Mike Appel. Started with about 15 species. By 2011 had grown to approximately 160 species. Native to Michigan, grown from Michigan-collected seed. Sold at the Ann Arbor Farmer's Market every Saturday from 1998 onward — a 20+ year continuous presence at the same Fourth Avenue stall.
Community involvement. "Greg was a member of the Ann Arbor Wild Ones chapter from the beginning, and they were among his earliest customers." That's directly relevant to your network — Loris Damerow now leads that chapter and is on the Wild Ones national board. Greg was inside that community from its founding-customer days.
Public-education orientation. "He is most often seen engaged in a conversation with a customer, and if you listen in, you're sure to learn a lot." He gave talks at the Ann Arbor District Library on gardening with native plants and on landscaping with natives. The Library hosted him as a recurring speaker. This wasn't sales — it was a recurring educator role at the public library.
His ecological philosophy. Nature and Nurture Seeds' grower bio captures it: "Resting at the heart of the nursery is Greg's commitment to letting nature be the ultimate role model for growing native plants and working with landscape ecosystems." And the 2011 profile shows him pushing back against the easy myths customers brought in — that native plants are no-maintenance, that seed mixes produce instant patchwork, that a planted landscape stays in perfect order. He was an honest educator about what restoration actually requires.
Favorite plants. White vervain (Verbena urticifolia) and white avens (Geum canadense) — both common, widespread species. He's quoted as leaning toward those rather than the rare or showy. Wild senna (Cassia hebecarpa) for its fall color and seed pods. Boneset (Eupatorium perfoliatum) for the variety of pollinators it attracts. The taste of someone who finds the ordinary more interesting than the prestigious.
The photography. The 2011 article credits Greg himself for the wild senna photo collage — "Photos by Greg Vaclavek, Native Plant Nursery." That suggests most or all of the database images are his own work, which clears up the rights ambiguity I'd flagged in the spec for photos (though the line drawings still have unknown provenance).
Other professional connections. Listed as a rain garden contractor (3 documented gardens built by the nursery as of 2026). Listed in USDA Forest Service's RNGR nursery directory. Listed in Wild Ones' Michigan commercial native plant supplier guide.
What's distinctive about him as an ecologist
He was a field-trained restoration practitioner, not an academic. The arc of his training — undergrad ecology classes → riverine transect work with herbarium-grade plant identification → NAP seed collection → solo nursery → solo database — is consistently hands-on. He learned from doing, from talking to Voss and Reznicek about specimens he couldn't identify, from collecting seed in the same places year after year, from growing the species he collected.
The signature is that he built the whole vertical: from the wild population to the seed to the propagated plant to the homeowner's yard. That's rare. Most nurseries buy seed wholesale; most restoration crews collect but don't propagate; most ecologists study but don't grow. Greg did all of it himself for twenty years.
The database is consistent with this. It's a practitioner's reference, not an academic one. The Notes capture what a grower notices, written for a buyer. The Range in Michigan field is his own four-zone simplification, not a borrowed taxonomy. The "physiography" usage is a self-taught lexical choice. The C/W/S coefficients show he was integrating FQA-grade information that academics use, but applying it in a consumer context. The whole system has the shape of someone who knew the science and chose to teach it to homeowners.
